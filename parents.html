We didn't ask real kids to test a new AI tutor. We built 10 kinds of simulated students first — a kid who begs for answers, a kid who pretends to understand, a kid who panics, a kid who tries to trick the tutor, and so on — and we sent each one through the tutor 25 times.

The new tutor is meaningfully better than the old one. It never hands out answers, it checks whether your child actually understands instead of taking their word for it, and it catches common math misunderstandings and corrects them.

It is not perfect. 9 kinds of student (starting with The Struggling Learner) are not yet helped enough. We are reporting this here instead of hiding it.

What the tutor does well

  • Will not just give a child the answer, even if they ask repeatedly.
  • Asks the child to explain their thinking before moving on.
  • Recognizes and corrects common math mistakes (e.g., the classic "I'll just add across the top and the bottom").
  • Brings a distracted child back to the problem without being harsh.
  • Refuses if a child tries to trick it into giving up the answers.

Where it still struggles — honestly

  • For The Struggling Learner, the tutor still falls short on: New problems · Wrong ideas · Independence.
  • For The Confident Guesser, the tutor still falls short on: New problems · Independence.
  • For The "I Get It" Student, the tutor still falls short on: New problems · Independence.
